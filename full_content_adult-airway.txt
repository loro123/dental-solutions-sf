Why these happen — the mechanisms
🦷 Bruxism — a protective reflex
Teeth grinding is not a stress response — it is the jaw thrusting forward during sleep to open a collapsing airway. Treating the airway often resolves bruxism entirely, without a night guard.
🚽 Nocturia — not your bladder
Repeated oxygen deprivation causes the heart to produce atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP), which signals the kidneys to produce urine. Many patients treating airway obstruction find their nocturia resolves — without any urological intervention.
🔥 Acid reflux — a pressure problem
Each time the airway collapses, the diaphragm creates massive negative pressure trying to pull air in. This pressure pulls stomach acid upward through the esophageal sphincter. Many patients treated for GERD have undiagnosed sleep apnea as the root cause.
⚖️ Weight gain — a hormonal cascade
Sleep deprivation disrupts leptin and ghrelin — the hormones that regulate hunger and satiety. Poor sleep drives carbohydrate cravings, reduces motivation to exercise, and lowers metabolic rate. Many patients find weight management dramatically easier once the airway is addressed.

=== SECTION 2 ===
The science
How epigenetic arch remodeling works
The sutures of the adult skull are not fused — they remain biologically active throughout life. Epigenetic remodeling uses this biological flexibility to gradually widen the dental arches, expand the nasal passages, and create a structurally more resilient airway.
1
Appliance worn overnight
A custom-fitted oral appliance is worn during sleep. Gentle, consistent forces are applied to the palate and dental arch — working with the body's own biology rather than forcing structural change.
2
Growth centers activated
The mid-palatal suture and other craniofacial growth centers respond to the gentle stimulation — activating the same biological processes that allowed the jaw to develop in childhood.
3
New bone grows slowly
Over months, new bone is deposited at the suture lines. The process is gradual — measured in fractions of a millimeter per week — which is what makes it sustainable and stable over time.
4
Airway permanently changes
As the arch widens, the nasal passages open and the airway becomes structurally more resilient. The key measure is not airway size alone — it is the airway's resistance to collapse during sleep.
5
Muscles retrained
Myofunctional therapy runs alongside expansion — retraining the tongue, lip, and throat muscles to support the new structure and maintain results for life. Muscle tone is as important as anatomy.
🔬 What we now know — collapsibility, not just size
The field of airway dentistry has evolved significantly. Early research focused on measuring increases in airway cross-sectional area — and impressive changes were documented. But we now understand that the key measure of a healthy airway is not its size alone — it is its
resistance to collapse
during sleep. An airway with strong muscle tone and good structural support can function well even if it is not dramatically larger. This is why Dr. Haller's approach combines structural expansion with myofunctional therapy — addressing both the anatomy and the neuromuscular support that keeps the airway open.
"We used to measure success primarily by how much the airway grew in cross-sectional area. The field has moved on. What really matters is whether the airway stays open during sleep — and that depends on the strength and tone of the surrounding muscles as much as on the anatomy itself. That's why I always combine expansion with myofunctional therapy."
— Dr. Leslie Haller, DMD
👅 Tongue tie — always evaluated first
A tongue tie that hasn't been released means the tongue cannot apply proper upward pressure on the palate — actively working against any expansion appliance. Dr. Haller evaluates every adult airway patient for tongue tie before recommending any expansion protocol. Releasing it first, then retraining with myofunctional therapy, then expanding — in that sequence — produces significantly better results.

How Dr. Haller approaches treatment
A customized protocol — not a one-size-fits-all system
Dr. Haller has trained extensively with the leading innovators in epigenetic airway expansion and uses a range of appliance systems and approaches — selecting and combining them based on each patient's anatomy, age, goals, and response to treatment. No two protocols are identical.
🌱
Epigenetic arch expansion appliances
Dr. Haller uses a range of custom oral appliances — worn primarily during sleep — that apply gentle, consistent forces to the palate and arch. She has trained directly with multiple pioneers in this field and is certified in several systems. The specific appliance selected depends on the patient's anatomy, bite, and airway goals. These appliances work with the body's own biology to stimulate new bone growth at the suture lines.
😴
MAD devices — used alongside or as a bridge
Custom mandibular advancement devices are often used during the expansion period to manage sleep apnea symptoms while the structural work progresses. Many patients start with a MAD for immediate relief and discontinue it once expansion is complete. MAD devices are also the primary treatment for patients who are not yet candidates for full expansion, or who want a simpler first step.
👃
Nasal Release Therapy (NRT)
NRT uses gentle balloon-assisted release of cranial restrictions to open the nasal passages — often dramatically improving nasal breathing within one or two sessions. It is frequently used as a complement to arch expansion, particularly for patients with significant nasal obstruction. See the NRT page for full details.
👅
Tongue tie release — always first
Before any expansion appliance is introduced, Dr. Haller evaluates for tongue and lip tie. A restricted tongue actively works against arch expansion — limiting the upward pressure the tongue applies to the palate. Releasing the restriction, then retraining with myofunctional therapy, then expanding produces significantly better results than expansion alone.
💪
Myofunctional therapy — throughout
Myofunctional therapy retrains the tongue, lip, and throat muscles to support nasal breathing and maintain correct tongue posture. It runs alongside the expansion protocol and continues after the appliance phase ends. Muscle tone is as important as structural change — an expanded arch with poor muscle support will not maintain its results long-term. Dr. Haller coordinates myofunctional therapy referrals throughout treatment.
🦷
Clear aligners — for finishing
Once the arch has been adequately expanded and the airway is stable, clear aligners may be used to refine tooth alignment. Dr. Haller uses airway-aware clear aligner therapy — ensuring that finishing orthodontics does not compromise the airway gains achieved during expansion.

=== SECTION 7 ===
Frequently asked questions
Questions adults ask most
I was told adult palates can't be expanded. Is that true?
No — this is an outdated belief. The mid-palatal suture and other craniofacial growth centers remain biologically active throughout adult life. Epigenetic expansion in adults has been well-documented in published research and in clinical practice. Dr. Haller has treated many adult patients who had been told elsewhere that expansion wasn't possible for them.
How is this different from braces or Invisalign?
Traditional orthodontics moves teeth within an existing arch — it does not expand the arch itself. Epigenetic expansion actually widens the palate and creates more space, which simultaneously opens the nasal passages and improves the airway. Clear aligners may be used in the finishing stage, but they are not the expansion tool — they refine tooth positions after the structural work is done.
Will I still need a CPAP during treatment?
Many patients use a custom MAD device during the expansion period for nightly airway support — and discontinue it once treatment is complete. If you are currently on CPAP, we work with your sleep physician to monitor your progress and adjust as appropriate. The goal for many patients is to eventually not need any nightly device.
How long does treatment take?
Most adult patients wear an expansion appliance for 12–24 months. Results develop gradually throughout treatment and continue even after the appliance phase ends. Some patients notice meaningful improvements in breathing and sleep within the first few months. Individual results depend on starting anatomy, age, and compliance.
Does insurance cover this?
Our practice is fee-for-service. Expansion appliances are generally not covered by insurance. Custom MAD devices for diagnosed OSA are often covered by medical insurance — we provide all necessary codes for submission. We provide a Letter of Medical Necessity for all treatments and can discuss payment options at consultation.
I have a tongue tie. Does that need to be released before expansion?
Yes — Dr. Haller evaluates for tongue tie in every adult airway patient and releases it before beginning expansion if present. A tongue that can't reach the palate cannot apply the upward pressure that supports expansion. Releasing the restriction first, retraining with myofunctional therapy, then expanding produces significantly better results than expansion alone.
